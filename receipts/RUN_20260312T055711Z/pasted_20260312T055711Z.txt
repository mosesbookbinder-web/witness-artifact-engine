FIELD NOTE — INITIAL PLACEMENT
Atlanta, 2025  Today I placed Ginger’s prints into the archive. I did not just record what she kept — I felt the reason she kept them.  She had two names because her life split early. She kept these prints because they remembered her before the split defined her.  I recognized myself in her. I am learning to carry my names in one body.  The record is still forming.

PREFATORY NOTE
Every case in The Bookbinder Archive begins with a moment of contact — the act of placing, observing, or recording before the object is cataloged. This practice acknowledges that the archive is not neutral: it is a living record shaped by the hand that keeps it.  The entry that follows was written at the time of placement. It remains unedited. It stands as the first trace of care before classification — the point where feeling becomes record and record becomes history.

THE FLOWERS COLLECTION — PROVENANCE & PLACEMENT RECORD
The Bookbinder Archive, 2025  Artist: Allan Robinson Medium: Three hand-pulled woodcut prints Date: 1965 Style: Southern Regional Figurative Expressionism Edition: Two numbered prints and one Artist’s Proof bearing the inscription “For Jan.”
Ginger Dorothy C. Flowers (1936–2021) lived a life shaped by movement, searching, contradiction, and self-invention. Born in a small Southern town where silence was a kind of social currency, she grew up learning how to carry two identities at once. Her own life reflected this: two names, two selves, one carried publicly, one held privately.  She attended university in Alabama and later pursued graduate study in Tennessee during the early- to mid-1960s. These were the years when Southern university art studios were quiet refuges for people who did not fit the expectations handed to them. Print shops stayed open late. Ideas moved faster than consequences. Lives began away from their families’ gaze.  It was during this formative period that Ginger encountered Allan Robinson, the printmaker who created these works. His woodcuts are not decorative. They are emotionally direct, interior, and precise. They speak in a language shared between people who recognize one another without needing to name the recognition.  The Artist’s Proof is inscribed “For Jan.” Jan was not Ginger’s legal name. It was the name of the self she stepped into when she believed her life could still expand. Someone saw that in her — and named it — and she kept that name.

CURATORIAL STATEMENT
The purpose of this archive is not to resolve a life into a single narrative, but to hold the record as it survived: in prints, papers, personal effects, and the histories that linger between them.  This case centers on the life of a woman known by more than one name — Dorothy, Virginia, and Jan — whose experiences crossed state lines, relationships, identities, and eras of American culture where private lives were often lived quietly alongside public ones.  The objects preserved here do not speak alone. They speak together: • A set of woodcut prints made by an artist she knew when she was young, signed and inscribed to her. • A divorce decree belonging to her mother, marking a rupture in the family that shaped her early life. • A set of Eastern Air Lines service flatware, carried through decades of work in the sky. • A photograph of the artist, taken years later, confirming that the world moved forward even after their paths separated.  These items, taken as a whole, trace a life in motion — a life defined not only by where she lived, but by how she loved, how she became, and what she carried until the end.  Nothing here is presented to resolve contradictions. People are allowed to have more than one name. People are allowed to be more than one version of themselves.  The archive does not correct the story — it keeps it.
